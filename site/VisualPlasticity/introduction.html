Introduction

There is much research into whether and how the functional architecture of visual cortex changes in the case of deprivation or atypical visual input (Hubel and Wiesel, 1977). However, the impact of deprivation of one sense on the cortical architecture of remaining senses is less well understood. The study of visual changes in congenitally deaf people provides a model to address this question. Deaf people have specifically enhanced detection of targets (Loke and Song, 1991), and motion discrimination in the peripheral visual field (PVF) (Neville and Lawson, 1987).  These changes are thought to be compensatory, as deaf people rely on peripheral vision to orient to new information (Merabet and Pascual-Leone, 2010).

There are 3 possible explanations of why deaf people have better peripheral vision, all of which may contribute to the differences observed between these groups. The first explanation is based on ‘crossmodal plasticity’. Crossmodal plasticity is when cortex of an interrupted sense is taken over by remaining senses. Specifically for deaf people, visual processing colonises auditory cortex, and its increased representation in these regions leads to behavioural advantage. The second explanation is based upon attentional accounts, whereby deaf people’s brains do not differ from those of hearing people, but where they allocate their attention to does. The reallocation of attention to the peripheral visual field results in their improved performance in this region of the visual field. Finally, ‘compensatory hypertrophy’ is the scenario in which the architecture of visual cortex itself changes to in some way improve accuracy in these visual skills. We will discuss evidence for each of these explanations in turn.

Crossmodal Plasticity

Using a neuroimaging technique to measure electrical activity generated by neural firing through the scalp (electroencephalogram (EEG)), deaf people demonstrated a greater amount of electrical activity from regions including auditory cortex in comparison to hearing people when detecting changes in dot motion in the PVF.

Attention-based accounts

Differences between deaf and hearing people’s vision have also been explained in terms of differences in the ‘gradient of visual attention’ from the centre of the visual field to the periphery (Proksch and Bavelier, 2002). Attentional load paradigms are used to measure the allocation of attention throughout different locations in the visual field (Lavie, 1995). This paradigm demonstrated deaf participants dedicated greater attentional resources to the peripheral visual field, at the cost of the central visual field (Proksch and Bavelier, 2002). The authors do not preclude changes at a neural level, but strongly argue differences in visuo-spatial attention are sufficient to explain differences between the groups.

Compensatory hypertrophy

Accounts based on compensatory hypertrophy of the visual system are not widely supported. Retinotopic mapping is a procedure using fMRI to define each visual area. Using this method, evidence of compensatory hypertrophy of the visual system was sought through measuring the surface area in each visual area and the absolute amount of neural activation in these areas (Fine et al., 2005). No differences were reported between deaf and hearing groups in these characteristics in early visual cortex (Fine et al., 2005). However, this study may have been underpowered: the surface area of early visual cortex can vary by a factor of 3 between individuals (Dougherty et al., 2003), and this massive amount variation between individuals therefore would be likely to obscure any differences between groups of 6 as used in this study. Furthermore, matching size and activation levels in visual areas does not rule out the possibility of changes in functional architecture of these regions. There are many other properties which may differ.

In summary, there is evidence that crossmodal plasticity and changes in attentional allocation in deaf people are able to contribute to the account for their improved peripheral vision. However, it is possible hypotheses based upon compensatory hypertrophy have not been explored appropriately. The explanation based on crossmodal plasticity and attentional differences provides no information on how visual information comes to be processed in auditory cortex. The visual system is hierarchical and has a high degree of structure. Thus we would anticipate that visual information in auditory cortex would be preceeded by changes in visual cortex.

Additionally, optical imaging techniques have revealed an increase in areas of the retina and optic nerve which process information in the peripheral visual field. These data strongly imply changes are present at the earliest stages of visual processing, and an account of increased sensitivity to the PVF in deaf people based on reallocation of attention and cross-modal responses is insufficient.

In this study we will use population receptive field modelling with fMRI in order to characterise visual cortex in deaf people. Population receptive field modelling is a way of defining each visual area and providing a statistical summary of the receptive field properties of neurons at each voxel (Dumoulin and Wandell, 2008). In this study we will model population receptive field size, where we fit a Gaussian curve at each voxel which corresponds to the amount of the visual field represented at this point.

We hypothesized there would be differences between deaf and hearing groups in early visual cortex in areas which represent the peripheral visual field. This would demonstrate evidence of compensatory hypertrophy.
